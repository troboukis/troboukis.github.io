Chapter Seven

Visualising Your Findings

A chart is not decoration. It is an argument. Every design decision — scale, colour, sort order — either clarifies or distorts the underlying data.

Part One

Choosing the Right Chart

Start from the finding, not from the chart type. Ask: what relationship or pattern am I trying to communicate? Then choose the form that shows it most clearly and honestly.

What you want to show Chart to consider
Comparison between categories Bar chart
Change over time Line chart
Geographic distribution Map
Relationship between two variables Scatterplot
Precise values for a small number of items Table
The same pattern repeated across many groups Small multiples
Connections and flows between entities Network graph

Resist the temptation to use a more complicated chart when a simple one works. Complexity is not sophistication — it is often the opposite.

Part Two

Scale, Normalisation, and Uncertainty

Three technical decisions most commonly produce misleading visuals.

Scale: does the y-axis start at zero? For bar charts comparing quantities, it almost always should — a truncated axis exaggerates differences. For line charts showing proportional change over time, the answer depends on context, but the choice must be explicit. Never truncate an axis without a label that draws attention to it.

Normalisation: are you showing raw totals or rates? A map of total cases in a country will show only where people live; a map of cases per 100,000 residents shows where the problem is most concentrated. Make this clear in the chart title, not buried in a footnote.

Uncertainty: if your data has a margin of error, show it. A single line or bar implies a precision the underlying data may not justify. Use error bars, confidence bands, or explicit language to communicate what you do not know as clearly as what you do.

Part Three

Annotations and Source Lines

Every chart published in a news context must carry four things.

  • A title that states the finding, not just the topic. "Hospital waiting times rose 40% in three years" is a title. "Hospital waiting times over time" is a label.
  • A subtitle or note explaining non-obvious choices: the time period covered, the geographic scope, any categories excluded from the chart.
  • A source line crediting the data provider by name, with enough detail for a reader to locate the original data themselves.
  • A methodological note if any transformation was applied — normalised per capita, adjusted for inflation, seasonally adjusted — stated precisely.
These are not optional extras. They are what distinguishes a publishable chart from a working spreadsheet. Without them, a reader has no way to assess whether to trust what they are looking at.

Part Four

The Reader Test

Before publishing any visual, apply one test: can a reader understand the main point of this chart without reading the accompanying article?

If the answer is no, the chart is not finished. Add annotations that point to the key finding. Simplify the design to remove elements that do not carry information. Or reconsider whether a well-constructed table would communicate the finding more clearly than a chart.

A chart that requires explanation to be understood has not done its job. The goal is not to reward careful study — it is to communicate at a glance.

Show the chart to someone outside the investigation. If they cannot state the main finding after ten seconds, the chart needs more work. This is the most reliable test, and the cheapest one to run.
