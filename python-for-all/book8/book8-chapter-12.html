Chapter Twelve

Publication and Follow-up

Publication is not the end. It is the beginning of a conversation — with readers, with institutions, and with the story itself.

Part One

Headlines and Social Media

The headline is not a summary of the article. It is the first and often the only thing most people will read. It must state the finding directly, accurately, and without overstating what the data supports.

Check the headline against the data before it is published: does it accurately represent the finding? Does it claim more certainty than the analysis justifies? Does it apply to the full population described, or only to the subset you actually analysed? A headline that goes beyond what the data support damages the credibility of the investigation far more than a cautious one.

The social media headline serves a related but distinct purpose: it must work without the article's context. It must be self-explanatory, it must give a reason to read that is honest about what the article contains, and it must not reduce a nuanced finding to a claim the article cannot support.

The headline is part of the investigation. Treat it with the same rigour as any other claim in the story. If you would not put it in the article without evidence, do not put it in the headline.

Part Two

Supporting Materials

Consider producing, alongside the main article, materials that extend its usefulness to different audiences.

An explainer is a shorter, plain-language piece that gives readers without background the context they need to understand why the story matters. It answers the question a non-specialist reader would ask before they could follow the main article.

An interactive is a searchable or filterable version of the data that allows readers to look up their own area, institution, case, or name. It transforms a story about aggregate patterns into a tool that is personally relevant.

A downloadable dataset is the cleaned data made available for readers, researchers, and other journalists to use and build on. It is also, in itself, a statement of confidence in your analysis.

Not every investigation warrants all three. The question to ask is not which of these would be interesting to produce — it is which would make this story maximally useful to the people it most concerns.

Part Three

Institutional Responses and Follow-up

When an investigation is published, institutions implicated in the findings often respond publicly: with denials, with corrections, with alternative interpretations, or with announcements of reform. Monitor these responses and be ready to report on them — quickly if they are significant, and with the same rigour as the original story.

Plan the follow-up stories before publication, not after. Ask: what will need to be rechecked if the institution disputes the data? What questions does this investigation open that a second investigation could answer? Are there angles that could not be pursued in the first story — for legal reasons, because a source was not yet ready to speak, or because a document had not yet been obtained — that may now be possible?

The best data investigations do not end with a single article. They generate a record — of what the data showed, of how institutions responded, of what changed as a result — that has value long after the publication date.

Keep the data alive. Datasets that were collected for one investigation often become more valuable over time, as new data becomes available for comparison. Plan from the start how you will update and maintain the dataset, and whether you will publish updates.
