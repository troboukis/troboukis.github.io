Chapter Nine

Writing the Story

Data journalism lives or dies in the writing. The best analysis, buried in inaccessible prose, reaches no one. The central finding must be clear from the first paragraph.

Part One

The Central Finding and the Lead

Before you write the first word, answer this question in one sentence: what did you find? Not what you looked at, not what dataset you used — what did you find?

That sentence is your lead. It states the finding, grounds it in evidence, and gives the reader a reason to keep reading. Everything else in the story exists to prove, qualify, or contextualise it.

A lead that buries the finding — that describes the dataset, the investigation, or the context before stating what was found — loses most readers before they reach the point. State the finding first. Provide the context second.

Write the lead last. You often cannot write the best lead until you have written the whole story and know exactly what matters most. Draft a placeholder, write the rest, then come back and write the lead with the full picture in view.

Part Two

Story Structure

A data investigation typically moves through six elements. They do not need to appear in this order, and not every investigation needs every one — but each should be present somewhere.

Element What it does
Finding The main claim, stated clearly and attributed to evidence.
Evidence The data and methodology that support it.
Example A specific case that makes the abstract concrete and human.
Counter-argument The strongest objection to your finding, addressed fairly.
Context The broader picture that explains why this matters.
Return Back to the human story or specific case where the article began.

Part Three

Where the Data Go

Data belong in specific places in a story, not distributed evenly throughout. Summary statistics that establish the scale of the finding appear near the top, early enough to anchor the lead. Specific data points appear alongside the individual examples they describe. Methodology belongs at the end, in a clearly labelled section that readers can consult without it interrupting the narrative.

Do not reproduce large tables in the prose. Link to downloadable data or embed interactive tables where space allows. Numbers in running text should be rounded, contextualised, and limited to what the reader needs to follow the argument at that point.

One number per sentence is usually enough. A sentence with three statistics in it requires readers to hold three numbers in mind simultaneously while also parsing the grammar. Break it into shorter sentences, or move two of the numbers to a chart.

Part Four

Plain Language

Every number in the story must pass a plain-language test: could a reader who is not a specialist understand this, in context, on first reading?

  • Prefer ratios and proportions to large absolute numbers: "one in five" is more legible than "21.4 percent" in running text, and both are more legible than "2.3 million out of 11.1 million".
  • Round where precision is not meaningful. €2.3 million is correct; €2,287,403 implies a false exactness and is harder to read.
  • Explain every statistical term the first time it appears. Do not assume your reader knows what a median, a confidence interval, or a regression is.
  • Avoid passive constructions that obscure agency. "Contracts were awarded" is weaker than "the ministry awarded contracts". Data journalism is about who did what — make the subject of each sentence do the work.
