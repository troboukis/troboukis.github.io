Chapter Eleven

Documentation and Reproducibility

An investigation is not finished when it is published. It is finished when anyone — a reader, an editor, a regulator, a judge — can follow your steps from raw data to published claim.

Part One

What to Keep

Retain the following materials from every investigation, indefinitely, in a form that a colleague who was not involved could navigate without guidance.

  • The raw data exactly as received, unmodified — in the original format and file name
  • The cleaned data after each significant transformation — not just the final version
  • Every script used in the analysis, with comments explaining the logic of each step
  • A data dictionary defining every variable and every coded value in the dataset
  • Records of every communication with data providers: emails, letters, responses to access requests
  • The cleaning log: a record of every decision made about the data, including exclusions and the reasons for them
Use version control. Store scripts and data dictionaries in a git repository from the start of the investigation. Version control gives you a complete history of every change and makes it possible to return to any earlier state of the analysis. It is also the standard tool for collaborative data work.

Part Two

The Methodological Note

Every published data investigation should include a methodological note — either embedded in the article or on a dedicated page linked from it. The note explains the investigation in enough detail that a competent journalist or statistician who was not involved can follow every step and reproduce the main results.

The note covers six things: where the data came from; how it was obtained; what cleaning and transformation was applied; what was excluded and why; what the analysis consisted of; and what the data does and does not allow you to conclude.

The methodological note is not a defence. It is an invitation to scrutiny. Write it as if you expect a knowledgeable reader to try to replicate your work — because the best ones will.

Write the note as you go, not at the end. A methodological note written from memory after the investigation is finished is less accurate and less useful than one assembled from notes taken throughout the process. Keep a running document from day one.

Part Three

What to Publish

Consider publishing alongside the article: the cleaned dataset, the analysis scripts, the data dictionary, and the methodological note in full. Publishing data and code serves two purposes — it enables readers and other journalists to verify your work, and it makes the dataset available for further investigation by others.

Some material cannot be published. Records containing personal information whose release is not justified by public interest. Data received under conditions of confidentiality from a source. Material whose publication would cause harm disproportionate to its news value. Commercially sensitive information obtained under a restricted access agreement.

Document the reasons for any restriction in the methodological note. "Some data cannot be published for legal reasons" is less informative — and less credible — than a specific explanation of which data, which legal restriction, and what steps you took to verify the restriction applies.
