Chapter Eight

Journalistic Verification

The data tell one part of the story. The other parts come from the people inside it. No data investigation is complete without traditional reporting.

Part One

What the Data Say vs. What People Say

A cleaned and verified dataset establishes what happened, numerically. It does not explain why it happened, or what it means to the people involved. Before you write a word, gather three kinds of testimony.

What do those implicated in the data say about the findings? Their response may add context that changes the meaning of the numbers, reveal a methodological problem you had not identified, or confirm that the pattern is real and known.

What do independent experts say the findings mean? An expert who has studied the same subject for years can tell you whether your finding is surprising, expected, well-established, or contested within the field.

What do those affected by the situation say about their experience? The aggregate data describes a pattern; the individual case makes it real. Find the people in the data and let them speak.

These conversations shape the analysis, not just the writing. A source who explains why the data looks the way it does may prompt you to recheck an assumption. Run the reporting before you finalise the numbers.

Part Two

Right of Reply

Every individual, institution, or organisation directly implicated by the data must receive the right of reply before publication. This means contacting them with a specific, factual summary of the findings that concern them — not a vague request for comment — and giving them a reasonable, clearly stated deadline.

Record every contact attempt and its outcome: a response, a refusal, or silence. A non-response, when clearly documented, is itself informative and can be reported as such.

Right of reply is not a legal formality. It is a journalistic obligation that protects both the subject and the story. A response can add context that changes the meaning of the data. It can also reveal that your finding was based on incomplete information — better to discover this before publication than after.

Send a detailed written request, not a phone call. A written request creates a record, gives the subject the time to consult others and prepare a considered response, and makes clear exactly what you are asking about. Vague requests produce vague answers.

Part Three

Legal Sensitivity

Some findings carry legal risk. Before you move to writing, identify any parts of the story that may require legal review.

  • Findings that name individuals in ways that could constitute defamation if any factual element is wrong
  • Claims that attribute criminal or fraudulent intent — these require direct evidence, not inference from pattern
  • Personal data whose publication may be restricted under data protection law
  • Findings that could be seen as prejudicing ongoing legal proceedings

Flag these points before you draft the story, not after. The cost of a legal review before publication is a fraction of the cost of a dispute after it.
