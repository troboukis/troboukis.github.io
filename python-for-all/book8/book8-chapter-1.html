Chapter One

Choosing a Topic

Every data investigation begins before you open a spreadsheet. A clear journalistic purpose — and a testable hypothesis — determines whether you have a story or just a dataset.

Part One

Why This Topic?

Three questions anchor any data investigation before a single row of data is collected: Why this topic? Why now? What would a successful story change or reveal?

The answers tell you whether you have a story or a research project. Without a clear journalistic purpose, months of rigorous data work can produce a document that no one reads and that changes nothing.

Write your answers down. They become the compass you return to when the data leads somewhere unexpected — and it will.

Write it before you collect anything. A one-paragraph statement of purpose, written before you touch the data, is one of the most useful documents in an investigation. It forces clarity early, when clarity is cheapest.

Part Two

The Working Hypothesis

A working hypothesis is a provisional answer to the question your story asks. It does not have to be correct — it is the starting point that guides where you look. Write it in one sentence before you collect any data.

A good hypothesis is specific and testable. "Public procurement contracts above €50,000 were disproportionately awarded to companies registered in the two years before the contract" is testable. "There is corruption in public procurement" is not.

The hypothesis will change as you learn more. That is normal and expected. Document each revision so you can show your reasoning if it is ever questioned.

The hypothesis is a tool, not a commitment. You are not obliged to prove it. If the data refutes it, that too is a finding worth publishing — and sometimes a more important one.

Part Three

Actors and Viewpoints

Map the people and institutions connected to the story before you begin reporting. Every group that appears in your data should eventually have a voice in the final story.

Who Their role in the story
Decision-makers Those with power over the situation being investigated.
Beneficiaries Those who gain from the current state of affairs.
Affected parties Those harmed, at risk, or excluded.
Critics Those raising concerns or proposing alternatives.
Independents Experts without a stake in the outcome.

Identify which groups are absent from or underrepresented in the data. Plan how you will reach them through reporting.

Part Four

The Public Interest Test

Not every dataset with interesting patterns produces a publishable story. Before committing to weeks of work, apply three tests.

  • Does this affect a significant number of people?
  • Is the information not otherwise easily available to the public?
  • Would a reasonable person say this deserves to be published?

If you cannot answer yes to all three, reconsider the angle — or the story itself. The public interest threshold exists not to limit journalism, but to ensure the intrusion that investigation entails is proportionate to what it reveals.

Interest to the public is not the same as public interest. Something can be widely read without being important. The public interest test asks whether publication serves the public, not whether it attracts an audience.
