Chapter Two

Searching for Data

Find out what data exists, who holds it, and how to get it — before you commit to an investigation that turns out to have no data.

Part One

Start with the Ideal

Before you search for what actually exists, describe what you wish existed. What variables would your perfect dataset contain? What time period would it cover? What is the unit of analysis — a person, a transaction, an institution, a geographic area?

Write this down. The gap between the ideal dataset and what you can actually obtain is your reporting challenge. Sometimes that gap is the story: the fact that data which should be collected is not collected, or is collected but never released, is itself a finding.

The ideal scenario is not wishful thinking. It is an analytical tool. Knowing what you want makes it easier to assess what you find — and to explain to readers what is missing from the picture.

Part Two

Breaking Down the Variables

List every variable your investigation needs, not just the headline measurement. A story about hospital waiting times needs the waiting times — but also the hospital, the specialty, the time period, the region, and a population denominator to make comparisons fair.

Variable type What it tells you
Main measurement What you are counting or measuring — the core of the story.
Identifiers Who or what the measurement belongs to.
Time When — essential for detecting trends.
Geography Where — enables regional comparison.
Denominators Population, budget, area — needed to normalise and make comparisons fair.

For each variable, ask: who collects this? Who publishes it? In what format does it exist, and how frequently is it updated?

Part Three

How to Get the Data

Data reaches journalists through several routes. Use them in parallel — a formal access request can take months, while a scrape of the same information might take an afternoon.

Route When to use it
Open data portal First stop. Free, public, often machine-readable.
FOIA / access request When a public body holds data but has not released it.
Scraping When the data is on a website but not downloadable.
Source When an insider can share records directly.
Interview When the data lives in someone's head and must be elicited through conversation.

Part Four

Talk to the Right People

Every dataset has a human owner. Find who publishes it, who maintains it, and who designed the methodology. A short conversation with the right statistician can answer questions that would otherwise take weeks to work out from the data alone.

Identify also the domain experts you need: specialists who can help you interpret what the data does and does not show. This conversation should happen before you draw any conclusions — not after you have already decided what the story is.

Ask the methodologist, not just the press officer. Press officers explain what an institution wants you to understand. The statistician who built the dataset can tell you what the data actually measures — and what it does not.
