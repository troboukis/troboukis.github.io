Bar Charts and Line Charts

The bar chart and the line chart are the two workhorses of data journalism. Choosing the wrong one is one of the most common visualisation mistakes. This chapter teaches you when to use each, how to build both with matplotlib, and how to add the labels and annotations that turn a raw chart into a publishable graphic.

Part One

Choosing the Right Chart Type

The single most important decision in data visualisation is not colour or font — it is chart type. The wrong chart type can mislead readers even when the underlying data is correct.

Use a bar chart when… Use a line chart when…
You are comparing discrete categories — parties, cities, products, years treated as labels. You are showing change over continuous time — months, quarters, years as a sequence.
The order of bars is arbitrary or ranked by value. The order matters and the space between points is meaningful.
Example: voter share by party in a single election. Example: unemployment rate month by month over two years.
A common mistake: using a line chart to connect discrete categories. If you plot voter share for five parties and connect the dots with a line, the line implies a continuous relationship — as if there is a meaningful "between" two parties. There isn't. Use a bar chart.

Notice ax.bar_label(bars) — it automatically places a text label at the end of each bar showing its value. This small addition often makes a chart self-contained, removing the reader's need to estimate heights against the axis.


      

        Sorting your bars: always sort bars by value unless there is a natural order (e.g., age groups, electoral districts in geographic order). An unsorted bar chart forces readers to search for the tallest bar instead of reading from left to right.
      

    


    

      
Part Three

      
Line Charts — Showing Change Over Time


      
A line chart implies continuity. Every point on the line — including the spaces between your data points — represents a potential value. Use it only when that interpretation makes sense, which it usually does for time series data.

Setting ax.set_ylim(0, 30) forces the y axis to start at zero. This matters for lines too: a y axis that starts near the minimum value can make a small change look dramatic. Starting at zero shows the change in proportion to the whole scale.


      

        When not to start at zero: for data where the absolute distance from zero is meaningless — body temperature, stock prices, ocean pH — starting at zero wastes space and compresses the variation into a sliver. Use your judgement and always show the y-axis range clearly.
      

    


    

      
Part Four

      
Highlighting a Point — Annotations


      
Sometimes a single data point is the story. Annotations let you draw the reader's eye directly to it without making them hunt through the chart.

ax.annotate() draws a text label connected to a point by an arrow. The two key parameters are xy — the coordinates of the point — and xytext — where the label text should appear. Both use the same coordinate system as your data.
