Book Four · Chapter Five

Summary Statistics

Numbers in a column are raw material. Statistics — mean, median, standard deviation, percentiles — turn that raw material into findings you can report. This chapter covers every essential statistical method in pandas.

Part One

The Core Statistical Methods

Every numeric Series in pandas has a full set of statistical methods built in. You call them directly on a column. Here are the ones you will reach for most often.

Python · Try it — all the basics

      


      

        
          

            Method
            What It Does
            Example From This Dataset
          

        
        
          

            prices.count()
            Counts how many price values you have.
            There are 15 items in the price column.
          

          

            prices.sum()
            Adds all prices together.
            Buying one of everything would cost €44.05.
          

          

            prices.mean()
            Finds the average price.
            The average item price is €2.94.
          

          

            prices.median()
            Finds the middle price when all prices are sorted.
            The middle price is €1.59.
          

          

            prices.std()
            Shows whether prices are close together or spread out.
            It is about €2.64, which makes sense because the prices range from cheap onions to expensive salmon.
          

          

            prices.min()
            Finds the cheapest price.
            The cheapest item costs €0.69.
          

          

            prices.max()
            Finds the most expensive price.
            The most expensive item costs €8.99.
          

        
      


      
Notice that the mean (€2.94) is noticeably higher than the median (€1.59). This happens because a few very expensive items — Salmon at €8.99, Beef at €7.49 — pull the average up, while the majority of items are inexpensive staples. The median, being the middle value, is more robust to these extremes.


      

        Mean vs median: When reporting a "typical" price, journalists often prefer the median because it is not skewed by outliers. The mean is useful when you want to estimate totals. For example, if the average price is about €2.94 and there are 15 items, then 2.94 × 15 ≈ €44.10, which is very close to the real total basket cost of €44.05. Always think about which measure tells the more honest story.
      

    


    
    

      
Part Two

      
Percentiles and the Spread of Prices


      
Percentiles divide your data into hundredths. The 25th percentile (Q1) is the value below which 25% of the data falls. The 50th percentile is the median. The 75th percentile (Q3) is the value below which 75% of the data falls.


      
The gap between Q1 and Q3 is called the interquartile range (IQR) — a robust measure of spread that is not influenced by extreme values.

Part Four

      
Finding the Extreme Items — .idxmin() and .idxmax()


      
Knowing the minimum or maximum value is useful. Knowing which row has the minimum or maximum is even more useful. .idxmin() and .idxmax() return the index label of the extreme value, which you can then use to look up the full row.
